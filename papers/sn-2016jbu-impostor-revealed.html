The impostor revealed: SN 2016jbu was a terminal explosion

Brennan, S. J., Elias-Rosa, N., et al. · Astronomy & Astrophysics 664, L18 (2022)

arXiv abstract arXiv PDF Journal (DOI)

Summary

Some very luminous outbursts were historically called “supernova impostors”: giant eruptions that mimic supernovae but were thought to leave the star intact. AT 2016jbu (also called SN 2016jbu) was long-lived and bright. This Letter argues, using spectral evolution and late-time behaviour, that the object is best understood as a terminal core-collapse supernova, not a non-destructive impostor event.

Why it matters

If we mis-classify a true supernova as a “failed” or repeat eruption, population studies of massive stars go wrong: rates, energy budgets, and progenitor models all shift. Showing that a borderline event really destroyed the star sharpens how we interpret similar long-lived transients in the literature. For students, it is a clear example of how late-time data can overturn an early label.
