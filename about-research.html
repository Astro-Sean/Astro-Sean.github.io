Home · About my research

Supernovae are among the most dramatic time-domain events available for testing late-stage stellar evolution. The question that drives my work is straightforward: how does a massive star reach the point of explosion, and how does that history translate into what we measure at discovery? By linking pre-explosion activity to emission at and immediately after explosion, we can test mass loss and the immediate circumstellar environment, and turn early-time signals into physical constraints that stay meaningful as surveys grow.

Over the past decade, wide-field imaging has shown luminous emission at the sites of some future supernovae—on timescales from years down to weeks before the final explosion—so that, when the right events are found early, there is a realistic window to observe the progenitor system before it is destroyed. In practice the limiting factors are often timing, localization, and fast, trustworthy classification for follow-up: pre-explosion emission is relatively faint (roughly −16 < Mabs ≤ −12) and has been accessible only out to about 150 Mpc for this science. Addressing that scale calls for end-to-end, reproducible pipelines, well-documented training data, and transparent decision rules so that transient selections can be trusted and acted on quickly.

My PhD at University College Dublin (2022) focused on supernovae and related transients, including supernova impostors—sorting genuine core-collapse events from luminous non-terminal eruptions. At the Oskar Klein Centre (Stockholm University), I worked on spectral modeling and precision photometry of interacting transients and presupernova candidates. I am now at the Max Planck Institute for Extraterrestrial Physics, studying supernovae, massive stars, and their final evolutionary phases with time-domain and multi-wavelength observations.

My published work has shown that information-rich precursor candidates can be identified and targeted with modern survey data (e.g. our Astronomy & Astrophysics Letter on SN 2023fyq, 2024, and related follow-up including SN 2022mop). Those cases combine ZTF light curves with spectroscopy to trace precursor activity, circumstellar interaction, and post-explosion confirmation of core collapse. I established the Pre-Supernova Targeted Observation (PRESTO) collaboration, connecting photometric alerts and host-association tools to follow-up on facilities such as the NOT, GTC, and TNG, and I build open analysis workflows where feasible (GitHub). Physically, I work with the same families of tools used across the community for massive stars and explosions—e.g. hydrodynamic and radiative-transfer modelling (MESA, SNEC, CMFGEN-class codes)—to connect observations to density, velocity, and shock structure.

Alongside research, I have invested in teaching and public engagement. As a teaching assistant at UCD I co-developed inquiry-based undergraduate labs and tutorials; as a supervisor I emphasized reproducibility, code review, and clear documentation. My outreach has included large public events (Astronomy on Tap, Pint of Science, a Nobel Prize Museum lecture), a widely read piece in The Conversation, and sustained school-level STEM outreach, including supervision of high-school student projects.
