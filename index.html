About

Summary

I work on high-energy astrophysical transients, combining multi-wavelength photometry, X-ray data, and analysis methods that connect observations to the physics of massive stars and core-collapse explosions. I am especially interested in precursor activity, circumstellar interaction, and how modern surveys let us probe the final evolutionary phases of massive stars.

I collaborate with the Zwicky Transient Facility (ZTF), the Rubin LSST transient and variable science community, and the eROSITA consortium, and I develop photometric pipelines and analysis workflows used in transient follow-up.

Research themes

Focus areas

My research sits at the intersection of stellar evolution, explosion physics, and time-domain astronomy. The themes below structure most of my current and recent projects.

Progenitors & pre-explosion activity

Precursor variability, mass loss, and circumstellar material in massive stars in the years and months before core collapse; photometric and spectroscopic constraints on SN and interacting transient progenitors.

Multi-wavelength transients

Optical/IR photometry together with X-rays where available to constrain shock interaction, circumstellar density, and emission mechanisms in SNe and related events.

Surveys & follow-up

Using ZTF, eROSITA, and Rubin-era strategies to discover and characterize transients, from classification to detailed follow-up and population studies.

Methods & modeling

Spectral modeling, precision photometry, radiative-transfer-oriented interpretation, and Bayesian methods to link models to noisy, irregular time-series data.

Previous positions. Postdoc at the Oskar Klein Centre, Stockholm University (interacting transients, presupernova candidates, spectral modeling). PhD (2022), University College Dublin — supernovae and related phenomena.
